---
title: On patience
date: 2026-05-03
excerpt: A short note on what patience actually looks like inside a tutoring session, and why it is harder than it sounds.
---

The word *patience* gets handed around a lot in tutoring. Parents tell me they want a patient tutor. Reviews praise patient teachers. Even my own site has the word in big letters on the home page. So I want to spend a few minutes saying what I actually mean by it, because I think the word has gotten a little tired.

Patience is not waiting quietly while a student stares at a math problem. It is not staying calm when a child says, for the eighth time in a row, that they do not understand. It is not the soft voice or the gentle smile, though those are sometimes part of it.

Patience, the way I think about it, is choosing curiosity over urgency.

Here is what that looks like inside a session. A student gets stuck on a long division problem. They have done it three times. They are getting frustrated. The urgent move is to step in, show the next step, and keep us on schedule. The patient move is to ask, "Where in this is the part that feels stuck?" and then actually wait for the answer, even if the answer is "I don't know." Because "I don't know" is almost never the whole truth. Underneath it is usually something more specific. They are not sure if a remainder is allowed. They thought they had to memorize a step. They are tired. They are hungry. They are embarrassed.

When I let curiosity lead, the student starts to feel like the lesson is happening *with* them, not *to* them. That feeling is what makes them try again next week. It is also what makes them, slowly, start to ask their own questions.

The hardest part of patience, honestly, is that it costs the tutor something. It is faster to explain than to ask. It is more efficient to push than to pause. A patient lesson sometimes covers less ground than a rushed one. I have made peace with that. Because the goal was never to cover ground. The goal was for the student to be able to do this on their own, eventually, without me.

So when families ask me what I mean when I say I am patient, that is what I mean. I am willing to slow down. I am willing to ask the question one more time. I am willing to let the lesson breathe, because that is how learning actually settles in.

And honestly, after years of tutoring, I have come to believe that patience is just love spread thin across an hour. Nothing fancier than that.
